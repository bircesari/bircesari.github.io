Beyond Good Intentions: What I Learned About Making DEIB Actually Work

November 27, 2025 7 min read DEIB, Organizational Culture, Analytics

I'll be honest, I started Dr. Tana M. Session's course on DEIB data and metrics thinking I'd pick up a few tips on tracking diversity numbers. What I got instead was a complete shift in how I think about making real change happen inside organizations.

The Wake-Up Call

Here's the uncomfortable truth: many of us have been part of companies that talk a big game about diversity and inclusion. We sit through trainings, we see updated mission statements, maybe we even celebrate when hiring numbers look better. And then... very little changes.

People still don’t feel like they belong. Talented employees from underrepresented groups still leave. The culture still feels the same.

Dr. Session gets straight to the root of why this happens: we're measuring the wrong things, or not measuring much at all beyond surface-level demographics.

What Actually Matters

The course drove home a truth that's obvious in hindsight but often ignored: if you can't measure it, you can't manage it.

But metrics shouldn't stop at counting heads. DEIB data needs to reflect experiences, not just demographics. The course highlighted the indicators that actually show whether people feel they belong:

  • Are people from diverse backgrounds being promoted at the same rates?
  • What do exit interviews really reveal about why people leave?
  • Do employee surveys show different experiences across different groups?
  • Are diverse voices actively shaping decisions, or just present in the room?

The Messy Middle

One of the most valuable parts was learning about the roadblocks to collecting meaningful data. Sometimes it's technical, disconnected systems, missing survey questions, inconsistent reporting. But just as often, it's cultural.

People are afraid of what the data might expose. They worry about privacy. Or they're simply reluctant to disrupt the status quo.

Dr. Session doesn't sugarcoat this reality. She acknowledges that meaningful organizational change is uncomfortable, slow, and requires persistence. But she also provides practical ways to handle resistance and keep moving forward.

Making It Your Own

What clicked for me was realizing that DEIB cannot be a one-size-fits-all program copied from another company. A tech startup, a manufacturing plant, and a hospital each have entirely different cultural dynamics and structural challenges.

The course gave me a framework for tailoring DEIB metrics to fit the actual state of an organization, not an idealized version of what it should look like.

It’s not about achieving some abstract version of “perfect diversity.” It’s about understanding your culture, identifying the gaps, and creating accountability to close them.

The Work Ahead

I’m leaving the course with more questions than answers, in the best possible way. I'm thinking differently about how to turn employee feedback into real, trackable goals. I'm more aware of the need to manage expectations around how long meaningful culture change takes.

Most importantly, I'm convinced that sustainable DEIB work requires both empathy and analytics. We need to care deeply about people while also having the discipline to measure progress with honesty and courage.

Dr. Session’s expertise as an organizational development strategist shines in how she connects these dots. This isn't theory, it's practical wisdom shaped by real organizational challenges.

Your Turn

If you're working on DEIB within your organization, I'm curious: What’s been your biggest challenge? Are you measuring the right things? How are you navigating the gap between where you are and where you want to be?

None of us have this fully figured out, but conversations like these help us move closer to meaningful, human-centered change.
